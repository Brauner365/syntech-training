What is CV Library?
CV Library is one of the UK's largest job boards and CV databases. As a recruiter, you have two main uses: posting jobs to attract applicants, and searching the CV database to proactively find candidates. The database search is where most of your sourcing time is spent.
Running your first search
1
Log in and go to CV Search
From the recruiter dashboard, click "Search CVs" in the top nav. You will see a search panel on the left and results on the right.
2
Enter your keywords
Start with the job title or core skill. For the Vinyl Technician role: type "vinyl graphics" in the keyword box. For the Welder role: try "TIG welder" or "welder fabricator". Keep it simple first — you can narrow down after.
3
Set location and radius
Enter Yateley or GU46 as the postcode. Start with a 20-mile radius. If results are thin, extend to 30 miles. If too many, tighten to 10.
4
Filter by last active
Set "Last updated" to within 3–6 months. CVs older than a year are usually not in the market. This single filter removes most dead leads.
5
Review and shortlist
Scan the results. Click any CV to preview. Look for: job title match, location, most recent employer, and last active date. Flag strong profiles to your shortlist folder.

Saved searches and alerts
1
Save your search
Once you have a working search string, click "Save Search" and name it clearly — e.g. "Vinyl Tech — GU46 20mi". This means you do not have to rebuild it each time.
2
Set up a CV alert
From your saved search, enable "Email Alert" and set frequency to daily. CV Library will email you when new CVs matching your criteria are uploaded. This is passive sourcing — it runs while you work other roles.
Delete alerts for roles you have filled. A cluttered inbox of irrelevant alerts trains you to ignore them — which means you will miss the important ones.
What to look for when reviewing a CV
Green flags
Job title matches or is closely related. Based within 20 miles. Active in last 3 months. Stable employment history. Specific tools/software named that match the job spec.
Red flags
CV last updated over a year ago. Too many short tenures (under 6 months each). Location is borderline — check commute. Vague about what software or equipment used.
Indeed for recruiters — two modes
Job posting (active)
You post a job ad and wait for applications to come in. Syntech uses Indeed for this on most roles. The INDC tag at the bottom of every spec tracks which applications came from Indeed.
Resume search (proactive)
You search Indeed's database of uploaded CVs. Available on paid plans. Useful when a role is urgent and you cannot wait for applications — you go and find candidates directly.
The INDC tracking tag — what it means
Every Syntech job ad posted to Indeed ends with a short code: INDC. This is a source tracking tag. IN = Indeed platform. D = Direct post (not via aggregator). C = standard campaign on our profile. It lets us see in reporting which applications came from Indeed vs other boards. Candidates do not know what it means — it simply sits at the bottom of the ad.
Always include the correct Indeed tag at the bottom of any spec before posting. Wrong tag or no tag = broken tracking. If unsure which tag to use, ask Zac before posting.

2
Enter your search terms
Use the title field for the job title ("welder fabricator") and the keyword field for specific skills ("TIG MIG aluminium"). Indeed also supports basic boolean in the keyword field.
3
Set location and distance
Enter the postcode or town. Use a 25-mile radius as a starting point — Indeed is weighted toward public transport so bear in mind some candidates may not drive.

What is X-Ray searching?
X-Ray searching is using Google's advanced search operators to search inside specific websites — including LinkedIn, CV databases, GitHub, personal sites and more. It is called X-Ray because you are looking through a site's surface to find content that is not easy to find via normal browsing. Many strong candidates have their CV or LinkedIn profile indexed by Google even if they are not actively on job boards.

Step-by-step X-Ray workflow
1
Open Google (not LinkedIn)
Go to google.co.uk. Paste your X-Ray string into the search bar exactly as written. Press enter.
2
Review the results
Google shows LinkedIn profiles, personal sites, PDF CVs and more. Scan titles and snippets for relevance. Click through on anything promising.
3
Refine your string
If too many results: add more specific terms or a -exclusion. If too few results: remove a term, widen the location, or try an OR variant.
4
Save the string
Copy working strings into a notes doc with the role name and date. You will reuse these for similar roles in future.
5
Add to Atlas
Any candidate you find via X-Ray should be added to Atlas ATS immediately — even if you have not spoken to them yet. Log where you found them in the source field.
Tips and common mistakes
Use Google's Tools menu to filter by "Past year" — this removes old results and surfaces recently updated profiles and CVs.
LinkedIn profiles found via Google X-Ray are public snapshots. The data may be out of date. Always check the actual LinkedIn profile before reaching out — their current job may have changed since Google indexed it.
X-Ray is most powerful for niche technical roles where CV Library and Indeed have thin databases. For the Welder Fabricator role specifically, X-Ray often surfaces candidates who are not actively looking but are findable.
The Syntech LinkedIn approach
Steve has built a LinkedIn network of 13,000+ connections — predominantly engineering and technical professionals across the UK. This is Syntech's most valuable sourcing asset. The connections are a ready database: before going to CV Library or X-Ray, always check whether the right candidates are already in the network. You can search connections using LinkedIn's People search with filters.
Boolean search on LinkedIn
LinkedIn People Search supports boolean in the keyword, title and company fields. Always use CAPS for operators.
Title field examples: "vinyl graphics technician" OR "vinyl technician" OR "graphics operative" "welder fabricator" OR "TIG welder" OR "coded welder" Keyword field with exclusions: TIG AND (aluminium OR stainless) NOT "structural steel" NOT recruitment

Using LinkedIn filters effectively
1
Location filter
Use the Locations filter — search "Yateley", "Camberley", "Farnborough", "Fleet", "Sandhurst", "Hook", "Bracknell". Add each town separately to build the right radius without overshooting London.
2
Industry filter
For vinyl/signage: try "Printing", "Design", "Packaging". For welder: "Manufacturing", "Mechanical or Industrial Engineering", "Automotive". These help cut out unrelated results.
3
Connections filter
Filter by 1st connections first — you can message them directly for free. Then 2nd connections — message requires InMail or a connection request. 3rd+ = must send connection request first.
4
Open to work filter
"Open to Work" candidates have actively signalled they are looking. These are your highest-conversion targets — message them first. However do not ignore non-OTW profiles — many of the best candidates are passive.
Messaging strategy
1st connection — direct message
Free. Short and direct. Name the role, location, salary and one specific reason it suits them. Ask a question at the end. Do not paste the full job spec.
2nd connection — InMail or connect
Send a personalised connection request with a note (max 300 chars). Reference something specific about their profile. Once connected, follow up with the message.
Template — first message framework
Hi [Name], I came across your profile while searching for [role type] in [location]. I'm working on a permanent [job title] opportunity with a [type of company] in Yateley — offering up to [salary], Monday–Friday days. Given your background in [specific thing from their profile], it could be worth a quick conversation. Are you open to hearing more? [Your name], Syntech Recruitment.
Do not send the same generic message to every candidate. LinkedIn's algorithm and candidates themselves can tell. Spend 30 seconds reading each profile before messaging — one specific detail makes the difference between a response and being ignored.
Connection request strategy
Building the network is an ongoing job — not just something you do when you need to fill a role.
1
Connect with target candidates proactively
Even if you are not filling a relevant role right now, connect with strong profiles in your target sectors. A future version of you will thank you when the right role lands.
2
Connect with clients and hiring managers
Every client contact you speak to should get a LinkedIn connection request. This keeps Syntech visible and is useful for reverse marketing.
3
Engage, do not just collect
Like or comment on posts from candidates in your network occasionally. This keeps your profile visible without sending a message — they remember your name when you do reach out.
LinkedIn limits connection requests to approximately 100 per week for standard accounts. Do not burn them on irrelevant profiles — be selective and targeted.
What is Atlas?
Atlas is Syntech's Applicant Tracking System (ATS). Every candidate you speak to, every client you work with, and every job you are filling should be in Atlas. It is the single source of truth for the business. If it is not in Atlas, it does not exist — you cannot trust your own memory across 10+ active roles.
Never store candidate information only in your head, a spreadsheet, or your inbox. GDPR compliance requires candidates to be recorded in Atlas. If a candidate asks to be removed from our records, we can only act on that request if they are in Atlas.
Adding a candidate
1
Search first — avoid duplicates
Before adding anyone, search Atlas for their name and email. Duplicates are a persistent problem. If they already exist, update their record rather than creating a new one.
2
Create the candidate record
Click New Candidate. Fill in: Full name, email, phone, current location, current job title, current employer. These are the minimum required fields — do not leave any blank.
3
Add the source
Always record where you found the candidate: CV Library, Indeed, LinkedIn, X-Ray, referral, or inbound application. The source field is critical for reporting — we need to know which channels are working.
4
Upload their CV
Attach the CV immediately — do not wait. Go to the Files tab on their record, click Upload, and attach the file. Name the file clearly: Firstname_Lastname_CV_[Month][Year].pdf.
5
Add a note immediately
Even before you have spoken to them: add a note explaining why you added them and what role you are considering them for. Notes are timestamped and searchable — they are your memory.
Leaving notes — the right way
Note format
Every note should answer: What happened? What was agreed? What is the next step? Example: "Spoke to Sarah on 15 Jul — interested in Vinyl Tech role at Yateley. Available in 2 weeks. Wants min £32k. Happy to interview Tue/Thu. Will send over CV for Zac to review. Call back Friday if no update by then."
Write notes as if someone else needs to pick up this candidate tomorrow. Because sometimes they will. A note that just says "Spoke to candidate" is useless.
Log notes same day — ideally within the hour. Memory degrades fast when you are working 10+ roles simultaneously.
Adding and managing client contacts
1
Find or create the company record
In Atlas, companies and contacts are linked. Search for the client company first (e.g. "Camberley Signs"). If it exists, open it. If not, create a new Company record first.
2
Add the contact under the company
Within the company record, go to Contacts and click Add. Fill in: Name, job title, email, phone, and LinkedIn profile URL. Always add at least two contacts per client — the main hiring manager and a backup.
3
Log every client interaction
Every call, email, or meeting with a client should be logged as a note on their contact record. This includes updates you sent, feedback they gave, concerns they raised. If you leave Syntech tomorrow, someone needs to be able to pick up the client relationship immediately.

What is reverse marketing?
Standard recruitment: client has a vacancy → you find a candidate.

Reverse marketing: you have a strong candidate → you find a client who would want to hire them.

This is how Syntech generates new clients and fills roles that do not yet exist. A well-placed reverse marketing spec can open a relationship with a company who had no live vacancy — because you showed them someone too good to ignore.
Reverse marketing works best when the candidate is genuinely strong, has a clear and marketable skill set, and has given you permission to represent them. Never spec out a candidate without their explicit consent.
Which candidates make a good spec?
Good spec candidates
Niche or hard-to-find skill set. Available soon (within 2–4 weeks). Willing to move for the right opportunity. Clean employment history. Clear, specific achievements. A salary expectation that is realistic for their skill level. Commutable to your target geography.
Not suitable for a spec
No clear specialism — too generalist. Unrealistic salary expectation. Long notice period (3+ months) without something very special. Poor presentation or inability to articulate their value. Has not given consent to be marketed.
Building the candidate spec
A spec is a one-page anonymised summary of the candidate you send to target clients. It makes them want to meet the candidate before they know who it is — you reveal the identity after they express interest.
1
Get the candidate's permission and full brief
Call the candidate. Explain what you are doing: "I think you are strong enough to market proactively — I'd like to send a brief spec to 3–5 companies I think would suit you. Are you happy for me to do that?" Get verbal confirmation, log it in Atlas.
2
Gather the key details
You need: current role and employer (you will not name these in the spec). Years of experience. 3–4 strongest skills or achievements. Specific tools, qualifications, or certifications. Location and commute flexibility. Salary expectation and availability date.
3
Write the spec — keep it to one page
Anonymise everything. No name, no current employer. Focus on what they can do and what they have achieved. Use specific numbers where possible. Write it as if you are selling the person, not describing them.
4
Get Steve to review before sending
All specs must be reviewed by Steve before going to clients. He will check: accuracy, tone, whether the candidate is genuinely specworthy, and which companies to target.

Tracking reverse marketing activity
Log every spec you send in Atlas. Note: the candidate it relates to, which companies received it, when it was sent, and what the response was. If a company replies with interest, that becomes a live client conversation — hand it to Steve immediately.
Every reverse marketing email you send is also a Syntech brand touch. Even if this specific candidate is not right, you have introduced Syntech to a new company. Some of the best client relationships start from a spec that did not place.
The golden rule
Clients who do not hear from you assume nothing is happening. Even a 30-second update call or a two-line email keeps the relationship alive and demonstrates you are working the role. Silence is the fastest way to lose a client — not a bad shortlist, but silence.
Update cadence — when to contact
When you have submitted CVs
Email the CVs and call 30 minutes later to make sure they have arrived and to talk through the candidates briefly. Do not just fire CVs over and wait — that is what every other recruiter does.
48 hours after submission — no feedback received
Call to chase feedback. Keep it short: "Hi [Name], just following up on the three CVs I sent across for the [Role] — have you had a chance to look? Any initial thoughts?" Do not apologise for chasing. It is your job.
End of every week — active roles
Send a brief written update for every active role. Even if the update is "still searching, expect CVs Monday." Silence over a weekend creates anxiety — a two-line update prevents it.
After every interview
Get verbal feedback from the client the same day as the interview. Do not wait for them to call you. "How did it go? Was [Candidate name] what you expected? What stood out? Any concerns?"

Managing expectations
1
Set realistic timelines at the start
On the briefing call, tell the client how long you realistically expect the search to take. If TIG welders in Hampshire with aluminium experience are genuinely scarce — say so. Better to set a realistic expectation early than to promise CVs in 24 hours and miss.
2
Flag problems early, not late
If you are struggling to find candidates, tell the client within 48–72 hours — not after two weeks of silence. "We are finding the salary is below market for this skill set — is there flexibility?" is a much easier conversation at day 3 than at day 14.
3
Never overpromise
Do not say "I'll have 5 CVs across by tomorrow" unless you are certain. An expectation set and missed destroys trust. An expectation set at 3 and delivered at 3 builds it.
Tone and professionalism
Right approach
Confident, direct, specific. Know your candidates. Know the role. Have an opinion. "I'd strongly recommend interviewing Sarah — she is the best we've seen for this role." Clients pay for your expertise, not just your database access.
Wrong approach
Vague, apologetic, passive. "I've sent a few CVs over, let me know what you think." "Sorry for chasing." "I hope this is okay." You are a specialist. Act like one.
Never tell a client a candidate is "definitely interested" unless you have spoken to them that day and confirmed it. Candidate availability and interest can change — always verify before committing to a client.
Why pipelines stall
Every recruitment pipeline has natural friction points where things slow down or stop. Most delays are predictable — you can see them coming and act before a candidate withdraws or a client loses interest. The recruiter who proactively manages delays places more than the one who waits for things to move on their own.

Escalation rules — when to flag to Zac or Steve
Flag to Zac when
You have chased a client twice with no response. A candidate is becoming unresponsive or is going cold. A role brief is unclear and you cannot get clarity. You are unsure how to handle a candidate objection.
Flag to Steve immediately when
A client mentions they are talking to another agency. An offer is being delayed by more than 48 hours. A candidate withdraws or accepts a counter-offer. A client says they are "pausing" the role. You receive any complaint from a client or candidate.
Never try to handle a client complaint or a withdrawal alone. These situations can be rescued with the right approach — but only if Steve knows about them in time. Delaying the escalation is the thing that loses the placement.
The counter-offer — how to handle it
A counter-offer is when a candidate hands in their notice and their current employer offers them more money to stay. It is one of the most common reasons placements fall through — and it is almost always preventable.

Handle it before it happens: during the candidate qualification call, ask directly: "If your current employer matches the salary to keep you, would you still leave?" Most candidates have a genuine reason to move that is not just money. Find that reason and remind them of it throughout the process. If their only reason to move is salary, they are a high counter-offer risk — note it in Atlas and flag to Steve.
Candidates who have already been counter-offered once in the past are very likely to be counter-offered again. Ask: "Have you been through this before?" If yes, ask how it played out last time.
Reading the signals — early warning checklist
If any of these are true, a placement may be at risk — act immediately.
• Client has not responded to CVs within 48 hours
• Candidate says "I need to think about it" after an offer
• Interview has been rescheduled more than once
• Client mentions "budget review" or "headcount freeze" mid-process
• Candidate's tone shifts — less enthusiastic than they were
• Role brief changes significantly after you have started searching
• Client starts asking about timelines in a pressured way
• You have not spoken to the candidate or client in 5+ days

The briefing structure
Start with what you know about them: "Based on your background in [vinyl/welding], I thought you'd be a strong fit for this."
Company in one sentence: "It's a well-established signage manufacturer in Yateley — stable company, good reputation locally."
Role in two sentences: key responsibilities only, focus on what they'll actually be doing day to day.
Package: salary range, hours, contract type. Lead with the positive. "It's up to £Xk Mon–Fri days — permanent."
Why it could suit them: tie their experience to the role specifically. Not generic — specific.
Ask the qualification questions (see below).

Can you commute to Yateley GU46?
Never tell a client a candidate "ticks all the boxes" and then ask the qualification questions later. Qualify first, submit after. A CV sent without qualification is a guess — and a guess that fails reflects on Syntech.

Who they are
Camberley Signs is a well-established UK signage manufacturer based in Yateley, Hampshire. They design and produce a wide range of commercial and architectural signage — including built-up letters, illuminated signs, vehicle graphics, retail displays, and bespoke fabricated structures. The business sits in the mid-to-premium segment of the UK signage industry, serving corporate, retail, and architectural clients. They operate both a graphics production line (vinyl, digital print) and a fabrication workshop (metalwork, structural signage components).
Yateley sits at the Hampshire/Surrey/Berkshire border — this is a strong hiring area for Syntech with good access to candidates in Camberley, Farnborough, Fleet, Bracknell and Sandhurst.
What we know about their operation
Graphics production
Uses Gerber Graphics Advantage (Spandex Composer) for artwork and cut file preparation. Operates Zund plotters for precision vinyl cutting. Produces vehicle graphics, retail vinyl, and print applications. Likely serves a volume production model with multiple jobs per day.
Fabrication workshop
TIG and MIG welding of steel, stainless steel and aluminium. Bespoke fabrication from engineering drawings — frames, housings, mounting structures. Finish quality is important — this is not heavy structural steel. Precision light fabrication for visible signage components.
